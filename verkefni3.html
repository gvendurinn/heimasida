The what and why

Last year I bought a Bussmann RTMR fuse and relay box for the Samurai. The problem is that it didn't include any mounting brackets. The only mounting brackets I found were out of flimsy galvanized steel and were very pricey. I already needed to make a 3D model for the course so I decided on making a 3D printed one. I started finding dimensions online and verifying them with my physical piece.

Bussman RTMR fuse box

3D modeling and printing

We are using Autodesk Inventor 2022 for this assignment. First things first we start by drawing the face of our fuse box and also the mating surface on the car. It might be a good idea to keep in mind that tolerances might not be to good on the fuse box and also the printed piece. I put an offset of 0.5mm just so I have some wiggle room. After one or two hours I was happy with the model and was sure it would be stiff enough. The model is finished so we have to make a STL file for the 3D printer. It's simple enough with Inventor through Enviroment>3D Print. We press Default Machine Workspace and choose an appropriate 3D printer type.

We then choose the quality of the print and the orientation of the part. The quality is going to affect the printing time drastically so lower Medium resolution was chosen. The orientation is also very important so I chose the largest flat plane on the model. Lastly I chose export to STL and saved the file onto my USB stick. I went down to Fablab and was told it needed to much material to make. It was around 300 grams and would've taken around 18 hours to make. So I want back home and tried to make it as light as I possibly could. I completely changed the design and it took around 1 hour. The model was now approximately 50 grams and it took only 4 hours to print. All in all the project took around 6 hours counting the time it took to go to Fablab three times in rush hour.
